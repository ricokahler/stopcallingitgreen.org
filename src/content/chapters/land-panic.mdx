The land-use objection to solar is not always fake. Farmland matters. Wildlife habitat matters. Nobody wants to wake up and learn that a developer turned the place they love into an energy project while explaining it is for the "greater good" in the least comforting voice possible.

But the national land panic around solar is backwards. America is not a country that has been delicate with land.

We have highways, suburbs, feedlots, oil fields, pipelines, military bases, and mile after mile of crop systems built around policies most people never think about. We have already made huge choices about land. We just do not call them choices once they become familiar.

## The Scale Check

The Department of Energy projects that ground-mounted solar will need about 5.7 million acres by 2035, around 0.3 percent of the contiguous United States. That is real acreage, but in a country this large, it is not the land apocalypse.

Compare that with corn. U.S. farmers plant about 90 million acres of corn each year. A large share of that corn is used for ethanol. USDA ERS has described ethanol as accounting for nearly 45 percent of total corn use and over 40 percent in recent years, depending on year and framing. USGS summarizes research saying about 12 million hectares of U.S. cropland - roughly the size of New York State - are dedicated to corn grown for ethanol. Not food on a plate. Fuel.

This does not mean farmers are dumb. Farmers follow the incentives in front of them: federal policy, fuel standards, and local economies. The target here is not the farmer. The target is the national story that makes fuel-corn feel normal while making solar panels feel like an invasion.

## Legitimacy Is The Fight

There is a good-faith version of the solar land objection: communities should share benefits, prime farmland is valuable, and decommissioning plans should be credible. Good projects should be able to answer those questions without hiding behind vibes.

But the bad-faith version treats solar as uniquely artificial. It appears whenever solar needs to be stopped but the speaker does not want to admit they just distrust the machine. Suddenly the concern is soil or beauty in a place where other land uses have been waved through for decades.

Apply the standard evenly. Apply it to ethanol, grazing, pipelines, suburbs, and transmission lines.

Technology Connections makes this argument plainly: compare disposable fuel land with durable energy-harvest land. A field growing corn for ethanol gets one seasonal harvest, then the fuel is burned once. A solar farm harvests energy every day for decades.

Corn for ethanol is legitimate because it got absorbed into farm politics. Oil and gas infrastructure is legitimate because we inherited it. Old energy gets to be normal. New energy has to be pure.

When someone says solar uses too much land, ask: compared with what? Compared with the land required to maintain the old fuel system forever?
