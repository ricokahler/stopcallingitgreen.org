Before we talk about gas, we need to talk briefly about coal: there is basically no good reason to build or defend coal as a future power source.

That is not just a climate statement. It is an engineering statement. Coal is a dirty solid fuel that requires trains, piles, dust management, ash disposal, heavy water use, and slow ramping. Coal was an incredible old tool. It is not a serious future tool. Anyone promising it is selling memory, not electricity.

America tried to quit coal and accidentally got really into methane with better branding.

## The Word Natural

"Natural gas" is a beautiful phrase if your job is to make a fossil fuel sound like it wandered gently out of a meadow. It sounds clean, domestic, and reasonable.

Nobody says "mostly methane." Nobody says "explosive fossil gas." Nobody says "the invisible heat-trapping molecule that leaks from wellheads, processing plants, compressor stations, and pipelines."

They say _natural_ gas. And the room calms down.

The bridge-fuel story went like this: renewables are not ready, so let natural gas be the bridge away from coal. Gas burns cleaner at the smokestack and can ramp quickly.

In the abstract, that sounds practical. In the actual world, a bridge has a strange habit: the people who own the bridge do not want you to reach the other side.

## The Bridge That Stayed

The first problem is the molecule itself. Methane is a powerful heat-trapping gas, and gas systems leak. If the methane leakage rate is high enough, the climate advantage of gas over coal shrinks or disappears. The bridge story was sold with a very convenient blind spot.

The second problem is infrastructure lock-in. When you build a gas plant, a pipeline, or an LNG export terminal, the spreadsheet does not say, "Thank you for using this temporary bridge; please retire me early." The spreadsheet says: operate me, expand me, defend me in policy, find more demand. A billion-dollar asset is a lobbyist with concrete.

The third problem is export logic. The United States became a massive exporter of liquefied natural gas (LNG). Once that happens, "cheap domestic gas" is no longer just a household story. It becomes a global business. The industry uses that infrastructure to argue that demand should stay high for decades.

This flexibility makes gas extremely powerful. To conservatives, it is domestic energy and jobs. To liberals, it is a bridge fuel. To utilities, it is dispatchable capacity. It gets to sound like pragmatism in every accent.

But pragmatism means updating when facts and costs change. It means noticing when a temporary justification becomes a permanent business model.

## What Gas Actually Does

Gas really did help reduce coal generation in the U.S. Gas plants provide flexible power and fill gaps when wind and solar output are low. A serious energy transition cannot just chant "solar" at the grid and expect physics to be impressed.

But true does not mean permanent. It does not mean we should keep building gas infrastructure as if cheap solar, wind, batteries, and advanced transmission did not exist. The bridge does not get to rename itself the continent.

When an incumbent fuel calls itself the practical compromise, ask what it is compromising you into. Are you solving a grid problem, or are you creating an asset that will lobby against the solution later?

Once you see the branding game with methane, the land-use panic around solar starts to look very selective. If Americans are suddenly worried about what counts as a good use of land, great. Let's talk about land.
