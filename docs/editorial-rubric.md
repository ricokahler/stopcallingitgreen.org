# Editorial rubric

This pass is for tone, flow, and trust. The book should feel like a person making a practical argument to a skeptical reader, not like a campaign, a dunk thread, or a generated essay with good sources.

## 1. Reader posture

- Assume the reader is skeptical, busy, and not stupid.
- Do not make the reader feel like the enemy for liking a truck, a podcast, a show, a farm, a job, or a familiar fuel system.
- Prefer "here is the question I would ask" over "how could anyone believe this?"
- Let facts create discomfort. Do not add extra scolding where the evidence already does the work.

## 2. Evidence posture

- Do not overclaim causality, funding, or intent.
- Say when a fact proves a direct link, and say when it shows a system, incentive, pattern, or useful terrain instead.
- Keep caveats where they protect the argument. Cut caveats that only make the prose look balanced.
- Do not add new factual claims without a source trail.

## 3. Tone

- Casual, plain, and occasionally funny.
- Angry underneath, but not ranting at the reader.
- Patriotic where useful: America should be strong, harder to manipulate, and good at building.
- No fake cool. No nonprofit voice. No academic throat-clearing.

## 4. Chapter job

Each chapter should answer one main question and hand the reader to the next one.

- Introduction: Why is the label a problem?
- Chapter 1: What happens if we look at electricity as a machine first?
- Chapter 2: What does "follow the money" require if applied evenly?
- Chapter 3: How did fossil fuels become common sense?
- Chapter 4: What did "natural gas" hide?
- Chapter 5: Why is the solar land panic selective?
- Chapter 6: What does fuel dependence look like on a map and in a household budget?
- Chapter 7: What is the serious version of the night/storage objection?
- Chapter 8: What does the China/transmission contrast reveal about building capacity?
- Chapter 9: What cultural lane makes electrification feel like capability?
- Conclusion: What should a reader carry into politics and daily conversation?

## 5. Humanize checks

Based on the Humanize Writing skill, every revised chapter should pass these checks:

- Structure tells: avoid repeated section shapes and neat takeaway bows at every ending.
- Significance inflation: cut phrases that make every point sound pivotal, crucial, or profound.
- AI vocabulary: reduce clustered words like leverage, robust, landscape, ecosystem, transformative, and underscores unless they are the clearest word.
- Grammar patterns: reduce repeated "not X, Y" parallelisms, forced rules of three, and "This is..." sentence starts.
- Rhythm: vary paragraph and sentence length. Use short sentences only where they earn force.
- Hedging: keep directness. Remove "worth noting" style filler unless it protects accuracy.
- Transitions: replace generic transitions with the actual reason the next idea follows.
- Soul: preserve the writer's opinion, mild humor, and lived frustration without turning it into a lecture.

## 6. Repetition budget

These phrases or moves are useful only when rare. Keep the strongest uses and replace weaker repeats:

- "not a personality test"
- "not a culture war"
- "old system"
- "that is the point"
- "that is the move"
- "leverage"
- "not purity"
- "the map gets a vote"
- "become harder to fool"
- "stop being unserious"

## 7. Transition audit

After edits, each chapter ending should make the next chapter feel earned. The transition may be one sentence or one short paragraph, but it should answer: why next?

## 8. Read-aloud test

Flag anything that:

- sounds like a press release,
- sounds smug,
- sounds like a generic AI essay with energy nouns swapped in,
- repeats a move the reader just saw,
- makes the argument feel smaller by trying too hard,
- or would make a skeptical reader quit even if the facts are good.
